Big Red Trade

Introduction

Timeline: September 2023 - November 2023

Working in a small group, we aimed to design a solution for students on Cornellâ€™s campus to facilitate buying and selling items
Our starting goal was simple: to understand the motivations, preferences, challenges, and needs of Cornell students living off-campus regarding buying, selling, and exchanging items.
Next, we planned to use this information in hopes of designing a platform or solution that eases the process, reduces environmental waste and unnecessary expenditures.

The Problem Space


We found that university students constantly face the challenge of managing their belongings within the constraints of budget, space, and time. The lack of a centralized, secure, and easy-to-use platform for students can lead to unnecessary expenditures, waste, and even added stress during already stressful times like moving or finals.

We got to work preparing to interview users, which meant obtaining a Human Subjects Research (IRB) Certification and creating an interview protocol. We created a consent form, a protocol and recruited memebers of our target audience.


User Insights


For our data analysis, we started with an affinity diagram. Since we were a remote team, we chose to use Mural to help us gather insights. Our original daigram was very large, after taking insights from over twelve interviews. We ended up condensing it into five main categories, which later became our key insights.




Persona


We created our persona based off the key insights of our affinity diagram. The goal was to make it someone who was a memeber of our target audience.


Prototypes


Low Fidelity

We started off by independenty creating out own low fidelity prototypes. Mine was constructed from copy paper and vellum paper. I hand-drew every screen and used vellum and dry-erase markers for user inputs. When it came to conducting a usability test, I realized how difficult it would be create an app from scratch without any of the developmenet tools and plugins I was used to. I started by identifying which tasks I needed my prototype to feature, and decided on the primary screens. Then I had to work out the flow and functionality between the primary screens, which meant more screens in between. Once I found a user, I acted as the "computer", changing the screens as they made their selections.
